Stellar Marketing Studio Meet Sarah / The person behind it

Anywhere's fine.It never is.

Hello, I'm Sarah. Before we get to the marketing bit, let me tell you about dinner.

It's nine o'clock on a Friday and four hungry people are waiting on me.

We're somewhere new, a city none of us really know, and the question has gone up: where are we eating? My phone is out. I'm six reviews deep, cross-referencing the photos against the menu against how far it is from where we're staying, and someone has just said, kindly, that they don't mind, anywhere's fine, honestly. They don't mean it. Nobody ever means it. And anyway, I mind. I always mind.

I'm the one who finds the restaurant.

Every trip, every group, the job lands with me and I take it more seriously than it strictly deserves. Because the gap between a fine dinner and one you're still talking about years later is usually small, and usually findable, if someone's willing to do the looking.

As it turns out, that's the thing I do.

Not just on holiday.

I cook for other people but almost never for myself. Tacos and ramen on request, a tiramisu if you've behaved. I take photos on a film camera and send the roll off and forget about it, then three months later an envelope lands and I get to be surprised by my own summer. On a warm evening I'll happily sit outside with a cold glass of white and watch strangers go past for an hour, quietly deciding things about them. I notice people. I always have.

For a long time I noticed them on somebody else's behalf.

I spent years doing marketing inside other companies. Big ones you'd know, smaller ones you wouldn't, a startup, a university, a proper mix. I was good at it and I learned a huge amount, and the whole time there was a small, stubborn thought I kept failing to shake. I'd first had it at university, in a class about starting your own business that I sat in once and then sensibly backed away from. Get some experience first, I told myself. It was the right call. The thought just waited.

What finally tipped me out on my own was a kind of irritation.

I kept looking at businesses, good ones, run by people who plainly knew their craft, and finding the same website over and over. The same stock photo of the same handshake. The same words that could have been written about anyone, by anyone. You could swap the logos and lose nothing at all. And I'd think: what is this actually for? A website that looks like everyone else's, a social account that says nothing into the void. That's not a small thing to shrug at. It's money and hours spent making yourself easier to forget.

Here's the part they hadn't quite clocked.

People decide about you before you ever get to open your mouth. They land on your site, they have a scroll, and somewhere in those few seconds they file you under worth it, or worth haggling with. The brilliant ones were losing that moment. Not because the business wasn't good. Because the marketing had never caught up with the person behind it.

The people I kept wanting to help were women.

Women running small, serious businesses. Brilliant at the actual work, generous with the people they look after, and somehow talked into believing that looking professional meant looking like everybody else. There's so much of that talent going quiet for no good reason. I found I couldn't leave it alone.

So now I do the looking for them.

I start with a long conversation and a lot of questions, because you can't find what makes someone different by guessing at it, and you certainly can't find it in a template. I'm slow about it on purpose. I won't hand over something that's merely fine just so there's something to hand over. I take the work personally, more than is probably sensible, in the way you take personally a dinner you chose for people you love.

The clients it works best with are the ones who care the way I do.

Proud of the thing they've built. A little obsessive about getting it right. We end up honest with each other, and we end up looking forward to the catch-ups, which is quietly the whole reason for working for yourself in the first place.

I still find the restaurant.

I'm still six reviews deep while everyone swears they don't mind. It just lands differently now I do a version of it for a living. The work, when you boil it down, is paying close attention to a person until the thing that makes them worth choosing becomes obvious. Then making sure the right people see it.

Anywhere's fine, they say.

It never is. That's what I'm for.
